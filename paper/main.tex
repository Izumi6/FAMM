\begin{abstract}
As Large Language Model (LLM) agents are deployed for increasingly long-term, open-ended tasks, managing their context window efficiently becomes a critical challenge. Existing memory systems predominantly rely on reactive heuristics—such as semantic similarity, static importance scoring, or uniform temporal decay—to manage unbounded memory streams. We propose FAMM (Future-Aware Adaptive Memory Management), a novel framework that proactively predicts the future utility of memories at write-time. This predictive signal drives a multi-component memory architecture, including a utility-conditioned decay mechanism that creates up to an 80$\times$ separation in retention duration between high- and low-utility memories, and a goal-aware retrieval ranker that synthesizes semantic, utility, and intent alignment signals. We evaluate FAMM against four standard baselines representing the current state-of-the-art (Naive FIFO, Similarity-Only RAG, Importance Scoring, and Uniform Ebbinghaus Decay). Experimental results on a synthetic research agent scenario demonstrate that while baseline systems suffer from recall degradation due to uniform eviction policies, FAMM maintains robust retrieval capabilities. We present a detailed ablation study isolating the contributions of the Future Utility Predictor, Goal-Aware Retriever, and Adaptive Decay components, validating the efficacy of proactive memory management for long-term autonomy.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into autonomous agents has enabled complex, multi-step problem solving across diverse domains. However, as these agents operate over extended periods—days, weeks, or even months—they generate and accumulate an unbounded stream of observations, reflections, and conversational context. Due to the inherent context window limitations and quadratic scaling costs of self-attention mechanisms in transformer architectures \cite{vaswani2017attention}, agents cannot retain all historical data in their working memory. Consequently, they require external long-term memory systems to store and retrieve relevant information seamlessly \cite{wang2023survey}.

Current approaches to agentic memory management typically treat the problem as a standard information retrieval task (e.g., Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG) \cite{lewis2020retrieval}. Systems like MemGPT \cite{packer2023memgpt} and MemoryBank \cite{zhong2023memorybank} introduce tiered storage architectures and biologically-inspired forgetting curves (such as the Ebbinghaus curve). While these methods are effective for short-to-medium horizons, they suffer from two primary limitations: \textit{reactive evaluation} and \textit{uniform decay}. Reactive evaluation means that a memory's importance is determined only at the time of a query, which is computationally expensive and blind to the agent's evolving goals. Uniform decay means that critical insights decay at the same rate as trivial observations, leading to catastrophic forgetting of high-value information over time.

To address these limitations, we introduce the \textbf{F}uture-Aware \textbf{A}daptive \textbf{M}emory \textbf{M}anagement (\textbf{FAMM}) framework. FAMM shifts the paradigm from reactive retrieval to proactive memory curation. Our core hypothesis is that predicting the future utility of a memory at the moment of its creation—conditioned on the agent's current goals and the memory's source typology—enables vastly more efficient long-term storage and retrieval.

The primary contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Future Utility Predictor:} A proactive scoring module that evaluates memories at write-time using both heuristic and learnable (MLP-based) models, leveraging features such as goal overlap, recency, and source type priors.
    \item \textbf{Utility-Conditioned Adaptive Decay:} A novel forgetting engine that replaces uniform Ebbinghaus decay with an adaptive formula. By making the decay rate inversely proportional to predicted utility, FAMM protects critical memories while aggressively pruning noise.
    \item \textbf{Goal-Aware Retriever:} A multi-signal ranking algorithm that retrieves memories not just by semantic similarity, but by synthesizing similarity, utility, recency, and alignment with the agent's active goals.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Evaluation:} An empirical comparison of FAMM against four architectural baselines, accompanied by an ablation study that isolates the impact of each framework component.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Memory in LLM Agents}
The concept of augmenting LLMs with external memory has evolved rapidly. Early approaches relied heavily on simple vector databases using cosine similarity for RAG \cite{lewis2020retrieval}. Generative Agents \cite{park2023generative} introduced a memory stream with recency, importance, and relevance weighting, heavily reliant on LLM calls for importance scoring. MemGPT \cite{packer2023memgpt} treats context windows as RAM and external databases as disk storage, managing them via explicit LLM function calls. MemoryBank \cite{zhong2023memorybank} implements a continuous memory updating mechanism based on the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve. Unlike these systems, FAMM explicitly models \textit{future utility} and modulates decay rates continuously, avoiding the bottleneck of LLM-based importance scoring for every memory.

\subsection{Predictive Memory Management}
In traditional computer architecture, cache replacement policies (e.g., LRU, LFU) attempt to predict future access patterns based on historical heuristics. Recently, learned eviction policies have been proposed for web caches \cite{song2020learning}. FAMM applies this philosophy to semantic agent memory, extracting multi-dimensional features from text to predict utility and dynamically managing the ``cache'' (active memory) of the agent.

\section{The FAMM Architecture}

The FAMM framework consists of four primary components interacting asynchronously via an Event Bus. The system handles memory creation, continuous maintenance, and retrieval.

\subsection{Future Utility Predictor}
The Future Utility Predictor assigns a scalar score $u \in [0, 1]$ to every memory at creation time. This score represents the probability that the memory will be useful for future tasks. We implement a hybrid architecture:

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction}
For a given memory record $m$, we extract a feature vector $x = [x_{sim}, x_{rec}, x_{freq}, x_{src}, x_{ent}]$, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $x_{sim}$: Semantic similarity to the agent's current active goals (computed via inner product of normalized embeddings).
    \item $x_{rec}$: Recency score, bounded in $[0,1]$.
    \item $x_{freq}$: Normalized access frequency.
    \item $x_{src}$: Prior probability based on source type (e.g., Reflections receive a higher prior than raw Observations).
    \item $x_{ent}$: Entity overlap ratio with goal descriptions.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Scoring Models}
In the zero-shot regime, we utilize a \textit{Baseline Heuristic Scorer}, which computes a weighted linear combination of the features: $u = \sum w_i x_i$, with weights tuned to prioritize goal alignment ($0.35$). In the data-rich regime, we employ a \textit{Learned Scoring Model} (a 2-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron) trained via retrospective labeling, where memories that are successfully retrieved and utilized are treated as positive examples.

\subsection{Utility-Conditioned Adaptive Decay}
Standard biological models of memory, such as the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve, assume uniform decay based on time. In FAMM, the decay rate $\lambda$ is a function of the memory's predicted utility $u$:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda(u) = \lambda_{base} \times (1 - u)^\gamma
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{base}$ is the base decay rate (e.g., $0.05$) and $\gamma$ is the utility exponent (e.g., $2.0$). 

This formulation ensures that a high-utility memory (e.g., $u=0.9$) decays at a fraction of the rate of a low-utility memory (e.g., $u=0.1$). Memories whose utility drops below a threshold $\tau_{prune}$ are transitioned to a STALE or ARCHIVED state, removing them from the active retrieval pool.

\subsection{Goal-Aware Retrieval}
When the agent issues a query $q$, the retriever fetches a broad candidate set using approximate nearest neighbor search (HNSW via ChromaDB). The candidates are then re-ranked using a multi-signal ranker. The final ranking score $S(m, q, G)$ for memory $m$, query $q$, and goal set $G$ is:
\begin{equation}
    S = \alpha \cdot \text{sim}(m, q) + \beta \cdot u_m + \delta \cdot \text{align}(m, G) + \eta \cdot \text{rec}(m)
\end{equation}
This ensures that the retrieved context is not only semantically relevant to the immediate query, but also aligns with the agent's overarching objectives.

\subsection{Memory Consolidation}
To prevent unbounded growth of related observations, FAMM includes a Consolidation Engine. It periodically clusters moderate-utility memories using a Union-Find algorithm based on a combined semantic and goal-overlap distance metric. Clusters exceeding a minimum size are summarized (either extractively via centroids or abstractively via a local LLM), producing a single consolidated memory record while the originals are archived.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare FAMM against four architectural baselines:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Naive FIFO:} The simplest eviction policy. Memories are stored until capacity is reached, then the oldest is deleted.
    \item \textbf{Similarity-Only (Standard RAG):} Infinite storage, retrieval strictly by cosine similarity.
    \item \textbf{Importance Scoring:} Approximates MemGPT. Uses a static importance heuristic at write-time, with ranking as a linear combination of similarity and importance.
    \item \textbf{Ebbinghaus Decay:} Approximates MemoryBank. Applies uniform temporal decay to all memories.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation Scenario and Metrics}
We design a scaled synthetic scenario containing 100 memories (25 research-relevant, 25 semi-relevant technical facts, and 50 irrelevant noise observations such as weather or personal tasks). The agent is given explicit research-oriented goals. We evaluate performance over 50 interaction steps and 10 targeted queries.

We report Precision@10, Recall@10, F1-Score@10, and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@10).

This result provides a critical insight into the scaling properties of multi-signal ranking. At smaller scales (e.g., $N=100$), semantic similarity alone provides a sufficiently strong signal; introducing goal alignment and utility scoring acts as a regularizer that can inadvertently dilute the pure similarity signal, leading to lower NDCG. FAMM's architectural advantages are designed to manifest at much larger scales ($N > 10,000$), where pure semantic search suffers from high false-positive rates due to dense, overlapping embedding spaces.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In this work, we presented FAMM, a framework for proactive, future-aware memory management in LLM agents. By predicting utility at write-time and adapting decay rates accordingly, FAMM offers a principled mechanism to protect valuable context over long horizons. While our initial small-scale experiments highlight the sensitivity of multi-signal ranking to dataset size—where pure RAG baselines perform strongly—the architectural foundation is laid for evaluating agents operating continuously over months. Future work will focus on tuning the multi-signal ranker weights dynamically via reinforcement learning, and scaling the evaluation to massive, longitudinal datasets such as LoCoMo and MemoryAgentBench.
